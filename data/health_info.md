# General Healthcare Knowledge Base

## When to call emergency services

Call emergency services (911 in the US, 112 in the EU, 999 in the UK) immediately for any of the following:

- Chest pain or pressure, especially with shortness of breath, sweating, or pain radiating to the arm or jaw
- Signs of stroke: sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech (use the FAST test — Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
- Severe difficulty breathing
- Heavy bleeding that won't stop
- Severe allergic reaction (swelling of throat or face, trouble breathing)
- Loss of consciousness or seizures
- Severe burns
- Suspected poisoning or overdose
- Suicidal thoughts with a plan, or suicide attempt

## Common cold vs. flu

The common cold and flu are both respiratory illnesses but caused by different viruses.

Cold symptoms typically include a runny or stuffy nose, sore throat, mild cough, and slight fatigue. Onset is gradual and fever is uncommon in adults.

Flu (influenza) symptoms come on suddenly and are more severe: high fever, body aches, severe fatigue, dry cough, and chills. The flu can lead to complications like pneumonia, especially in older adults, young children, and people with chronic conditions.

Most colds resolve on their own in 7-10 days. Flu typically lasts 1-2 weeks. Rest, fluids, and over-the-counter symptom relief are the mainstays of self-care. See a doctor if symptoms worsen, last beyond expected duration, or include high fever, difficulty breathing, or chest pain.

## Hydration basics

A general guideline is around 2-3 liters of fluids per day for adults, but actual needs vary with body size, activity level, climate, and health conditions. Signs of dehydration include dark yellow urine, dry mouth, fatigue, dizziness, and headache. Increase fluid intake during exercise, hot weather, illness with fever or vomiting, and breastfeeding.

## Sleep recommendations

Adults generally need 7-9 hours of sleep per night. Teenagers need 8-10 hours. Children 6-12 need 9-12 hours. Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to weakened immunity, weight gain, mood disorders, cardiovascular disease, and impaired cognition. Good sleep hygiene includes a consistent schedule, a cool dark room, limiting screens before bed, and avoiding caffeine in the afternoon.

## Blood pressure ranges

Normal blood pressure for adults is below 120/80 mmHg. Elevated is 120-129/below 80. Stage 1 hypertension is 130-139/80-89. Stage 2 hypertension is 140+/90+. A hypertensive crisis is above 180/120 and requires immediate medical attention. These numbers are general guidelines; a clinician should interpret your readings in the context of your overall health.

## When to see a doctor for a fever

For adults, see a doctor if fever is above 39.4°C (103°F), lasts more than 3 days, or is accompanied by severe headache, stiff neck, rash, persistent vomiting, difficulty breathing, chest pain, abdominal pain, or confusion. For infants under 3 months, any fever above 38°C (100.4°F) is a reason to seek immediate medical attention.

## Mental health resources

If you are struggling with mental health, you are not alone and help is available. Talk to your primary care doctor, a licensed therapist, or a trusted person in your life. Many countries have crisis hotlines available 24/7. In the US, you can call or text 988 to reach the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. In the UK, Samaritans can be reached at 116 123. For other countries, search for local crisis support services.

## Medication safety

Always take medications as prescribed. Never share prescription medications. Check expiration dates. Read labels for warnings about driving, alcohol, and food interactions. Tell every healthcare provider about all medications and supplements you take, including over-the-counter products. If you miss a dose, follow the package instructions or ask a pharmacist — do not double up unless explicitly told to.

## Vaccinations

Routine vaccinations are one of the most effective ways to prevent serious infectious diseases. Recommended adult vaccines often include annual influenza, Tdap (tetanus/diphtheria/pertussis) every 10 years, COVID-19 boosters per current guidance, shingles for adults 50+, and pneumococcal vaccines for adults 65+ or those with certain conditions. Travel to certain regions may require additional vaccines. Check with your doctor or local health authority for current recommendations specific to your situation.
